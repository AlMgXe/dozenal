dec and doz: A Tutorial

If you want to convert decimal to dozenal or vice versa, then doz and dec are your friends. They can convert many numbers in many formats using a variety of transdecimal characters and can output to user-specified precision.

Limitations

As powerful as they are, however, doz and dec do have their limitations. Most specifically, they are not "arbitrary precision," in that they are limited in the range of numbers they can accept. Essentially, they work with the double-precision floating-point numbers offered by your machine. This limitation is, however, very rarely a problem. For example, on the low-powered machine I'm currently working on, the lowest available number is 2;9816e-210, and the highest is 4;X617e1E9. It's hard to imagine a real-world situation, short of some mathematician experiments, where this will be particularly limiting.

Basic operation

dec and doz are command-line programs, meaning that you operate with them by typing commands and getting responses. For these examples, $> will mean the prompt, where you type your commands; a line without a prompt are the responses you get.

Most simply, you just type the command, then the number which you want converted:

$> doz 1492
X44
$> doz 1492.33333333333333333
X44;4
$> dec X44
1492
$> dec "X44;4"
1492.3333

Notice that the last example has the number surrounded by quotation marks. Most systems will interpret a semicolon as the end of statement, so you need to enclose numbers containing semicolons in quotations to prevent this. Otherwise, you'll likely get a funny error:

$> dec X44;4
0
bash: 4: command not found

This, obviously, is not what you expect or want.

You can enter your numbers in "scientific notation" (more properly called exponential notation), as well, in the normal computerized way: end your mantissa with a lowercase "e", then put in the exponent:

$> doz 1.492e3
X44
$> dec "X;44e2"
1492.0000

Advanced operation

If you want your result to be in exponential notation, you need to add a flag. Flags are special markers that are put in a command; for dec and doz, that means before the number that you want converted.

So to get exponential notation in your output, use the -e flag:

$> doz -e 1.492e3
X;44e2
$> dec -e "X;44e2"
1.4920e+03

You might find, eventually, that you don't get the results you're looking for, and exponential notation could be the answer:

$> doz 4.539e-14
0;0000
$> doz -e 4.539e-14
4;X33Xe-11

You can also get variable amounts of precision, with "precision" defined as "number of digits after the radix point." To do this, you use the flag -k ("k" for "krecision"! (actually, this is designed for compatibility with the well-known Unix program dc)), followed by the number of digits after the radix point that you want:

$> doz 4.539e-14
0;0000
$> doz -k14 4.539e-14
0;0000000000004X33
$> doz -e 4.539e-14
4;X33Xe-11

Keep in mind that the precision is not arbitrary; the exact number that you can actually expect to get accurately varies depending on your computer, but it's typically around 14 (a dozen four).

Transdecimal Digits

dec always outputs using "X" for ten and "E" for eleven; if you don't like this, you can always filter your output through dozpret and use whatever transdecimals you want.

doz is more flexible. It will accept "X", "x", "A", "a", "T", and "t" for ten, and it will accept "E", "B", and "b" for eleven. Notice that "e" is not included, because it is used for exponential notation.

You can mix these digits however you want:

$> dec XxEtTaABb
4693695407.0000

And that's more or less all there is to using dec and doz.
